Your new setup · what's really going on

You didn't lose distance. And you didn't lose a club.

Two things feel off about the new T100s: they seem shorter, and it looks like the bottom of the bag is missing a club. Neither is actually true. Here's the real story — with the numbers.

Worry #1 · "My irons got shorter"

The number is a sticker. Loft is the club.

Loft is the angle on the face — the biggest thing the club controls for how far and high the ball goes, way more than the number stamped on the bottom. Two irons can both say "7" and have different lofts, so they fly different distances from the same swing.

Think of it like

Clothing sizes. A "Medium" tee isn't an exact measurement — one brand's Medium fits like another's Large. Iron numbers are the same. The "7" on a distance iron has way less loft than the "7" on a tour iron like the T100. Same name, different club.

The one number to remember

Your new 7-iron is 33°. Your old 8-iron was 34°. Same loft. So your new 7 flies like your old 8 — that 3° is the whole "it got shorter" feeling. Drop down one club for the same shot and it disappears.

Also part of the story

The shafts changed too — toward control.

It's not only loft. The old irons had a lighter, easy-launch shaft; the new ones have a tour-weight shaft that rewards rhythm over muscle.

Old irons
KBS Tour Lite
Lighter steel, higher launch, easy distance — built for a smooth, moderate tempo.
New irons
AMT Tour White · Stiff
Titleist's stock tour shaft. "Ascending mass": lighter in the long irons (helps launch the 5-iron) and heavier in the scoring irons (more control).

A tour-weight, stiffer shaft is more stable and repeatable — but it asks for good tempo. Swing it smooth and it flies on a rope. Try to muscle it and you'll lose both contact and distance. So if the mid-irons feel a touch shorter at first, that's the heavier shaft plus the weaker loft, both pointing the same direction: trading easy distance for tour-level control.

Bottom line

Either way, there's no hole in your bag. The biggest step is 5–6° at the very top, which is normal up there. The whole top of the bag was gapped on purpose. The exact hybrid pair is stamped on the hosel sleeve (and usually the headcover) if you want to be sure.

What actually sends it

Where distance really comes from.

In order of how much they matter:

  1. Hitting the center of the face
    The #1 factor — and it matters more with T100s, because the sweet spot is smaller. A flush strike beats a hard one every time. A toe or thin miss bleeds yards fast.
  2. Clubhead speed
    Comes from tempo and sequence — a smooth transition, using the ground in the right order — not from muscling it. This matters even more now with the heavier tour shaft: smooth wins.
  3. Loft
    Already built into the club. Set the moment you pick which club to pull.
  4. Ball speed
    The result of a centered strike plus clubhead speed. Get those two right and it takes care of itself.
"Getting stronger" is real, but it's near the bottom of this list and it's slow. Swinging harder for yards usually wrecks contact — and an off-center T100 loses distance fast. You end up shorter and more crooked. The opposite of the goal.
Why the pros choose these

"Weaker" lofts win tournaments.

The T100 is the most-played iron on the PGA Tour and the DP World Tour. The best ball-strikers on Earth pick these tour lofts on purpose.

A 33° 7-iron launches higher, spins more, and stops on the green. A jacked distance 7-iron flown 165 can't hold a firm Sunday pin — it lands hot and skips off the back. Into greens, control beats raw yards, and control is what saves shots.

Where to put your energy now

Start with tempo — here's what that even means.

Tempo is the rhythm of your swing — how the timing of the backswing relates to the downswing. It's not the same as speed. A swing can be quick or slow and still have great tempo; what matters is that it's smooth, balanced, and the same every time.

Here's the cool part: nearly every great player shares one thing — their backswing takes about three times as long as their downswing. A 3-to-1 feel. Long and unhurried going back, then a free, accelerating move through the ball.

Think of it like

A song with a steady beat. You can't rush the chorus and still have it sound right — it has its own timing. A golf swing is the same: find the beat, then repeat the beat. A simple cue is counting "one…two…three" going back and "one" coming down.

Five simple ways to build it:

Hold your finish
After every shot, freeze your finish for three full seconds — balanced, weight on your lead foot, buckle to the target. If you can't hold it, you swung too hard. This one drill trains tempo and balance at once, and it's the easiest thing to self-check on the range or the course.
Feet together
Hit easy shots with your feet almost touching. You literally can't overswing without losing your balance, so your body finds a smooth tempo — and the center of the face — on its own. A great warm-up before every session.
Swing at 80%
Practice your stock shots at about three-quarter effort. Most players hit it the same distance with better contact — proof that smooth beats hard. Owning a "smooth 7-iron" you can trust under pressure is worth more than a hero swing.
Count it, or use a metronome
Say the beat out loud, or use a free tempo app set to that 3-to-1 ratio. Syncing to an outside beat builds a repeatable rhythm faster than just trying to feel smooth.
The swoosh drill
Flip a club upside down and hold it by the head. Swing and listen: the loudest "swoosh" should come at or just past where the ball would be — not at the top. That means you're saving your speed for impact instead of throwing it away early. It's how speed and tempo work together.
The short list

What matters most right now.

The gear is dialed in. Here's where the real gains are this season — roughly in order of payoff:

  1. Center-face contact
    Spray a little foot powder on the face, or use impact tape, and look at where you're actually striking it. Tightening that pattern adds more distance and consistency than anything else — and it's the direct fix for "it feels shorter."
  2. Learn your real numbers
    Spend a session on a launch monitor and write down your carry distance for every club with the new setup. A yardage chart turns "I lost distance" into facts you can trust over a shot. Re-check it every few weeks — as you grow and your tempo sharpens, the numbers climb on their own.
  3. Wedges and the short game
    With the new gap wedge and matched shafts, learn your partial-swing carries — full, three-quarter, and half with each wedge. Shots from inside 100 yards and around the green are where rounds are actually won. It's the highest payoff per hour of practice, full stop.
  4. Tempo and balance
    Use the drills above. A repeatable, balanced swing is what makes good contact repeatable when it counts — it's the foundation everything else sits on, and it's free.
  5. Speed — as its own project
    If you want more raw distance, build it with structured speed training, kept separate from hitting irons. Don't chase yards by swinging your irons harder; that just costs you contact. Let real speed work — plus getting older and taller — do that job.
If you do one thing

Spend a session learning your real carry numbers and checking your strike pattern. The day those new numbers feel normal is the day the new clubs stop feeling like a downgrade — because they never were one.

What to do with this

Don't chase the number. Chase your number.

Learn your real T100 carries, trust your gaps, and pull the club the shot needs — whatever number is on the bottom. Speed will come from cleaner tempo and from simply getting older and taller; the distance shows up on its own.

The control you're building right now is the hard part. And you're already holding it.
