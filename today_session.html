Baseline Assessment

Afternoon · ~2 hrs · Range & Short Game

Today's Mission

This is NOT a normal practice session. The goal today is to collect baseline data — not to fix anything, not to grind, not to beat balls. You're a scout gathering intel on your own game.

Record honest numbers. The plan we build all summer depends on what you write down today.

Tap "How to" under any exercise if you need a step-by-step breakdown.

⚠ Heat protocol: 32+ oz water, electrolytes, hat, sunscreen. Drink steadily, not just when thirsty.
Station 1
Warmup
10 minutes · Putting Green

Start every session here. Wake up the body, calibrate feel.

About 3-4 min total:
  1. Leg swings — 10 each leg, each direction. Stand next to a fence or post for balance. Swing one leg forward and back like a pendulum, then side to side. Switch legs.
  2. Arm circles — 10 forward, 10 backward. Both arms out to your sides, make slow controlled circles. Start small, get bigger.
  3. Torso rotations — 10 each side. Stand tall, hold a club across your chest. Rotate as far as you can each direction.
  4. Hip openers — 5 each side. Lift your knee up to waist height, then rotate it open to the side like opening a gate. Switch legs.
Why: Cold muscles tear. This wakes everything up safely and gets the rotational range of motion you need for the swing.
What to do:
  • Drop a few balls 3-6 feet from any hole.
  • Putt them. Don't keep score. Don't analyze stroke mechanics.
  • Goal: feel the green speed, hear the ball drop, get your putter moving freely.
Why: Calibrates your touch before the actual test in Station 2.
What to do:
  • Move to a spot at least 30 feet from a hole.
  • Putt 10 balls. Try to die them in.
  • No scoring, no counting. Just feel the distance.
Why: Long putt feel is the hardest thing to dial in cold. Better to spend 2 minutes here than 3-putt the first hole of every round.
Station 2
Putting Baseline
20 minutes · Putting Green

Measure where you actually are. Use one ball, hole it out style — like the course.

The "clock face" drill:
  1. Pick a hole. Place a tee or ball-marker 3 feet away at the 12 o'clock position.
  2. Place markers at 3, 6, and 9 o'clock — all exactly 3 feet from the hole.
  3. You now have a clock face around the hole at 3 ft.
  4. Putt one ball from each position. Do it twice around plus 2 extras to hit 10 total.
  5. Full pre-shot routine each time. Count how many of 10 you make.
Why 3 feet: These are the "must makes." A tour pro makes ~96% of these. Every miss inside 5 feet is a stroke given away.
Same idea, longer:
  1. Set up a 6-foot clock face — markers at 12, 3, 6, 9 o'clock.
  2. 10 putts total, one ball at a time, full routine.
  3. Count makes.
Why 6 feet: This is the "make-or-break" range. Tour pros make ~70% from here. This is where putters become magicians or end up in the trees.
What to do:
  1. Pace off 20 feet from a hole. (One pace ≈ 3 feet for most people.)
  2. Hit 5 putts. For each one, look at how close it stopped to the hole. Count how many stopped within a 3-foot radius (about half a putter length).
  3. Move to 30 feet. Repeat. Then 40 feet.
  4. Total: 15 putts. Write the number that finished inside 3 ft.
Why: Lag putting is about distance control, not aim. Three-putts almost always come from leaving the first putt outside 3 feet.
Coach's note: Don't compare yourself to tour numbers yet — just record honestly. We need the truth to build the plan.
Station 3
Short Game Baseline
30 minutes · Chipping Green

The scoring zone. 60% of strokes happen inside 100 yds. We need to know where you stand here more than anywhere else.

What to do:
  1. Find a flat spot 10-15 feet off the edge of the green. Pick a hole that's about 20-30 feet away on the green.
  2. Use ONE club for all 10 chips. Pick your 52° (gap wedge) or 56° (sand wedge).
  3. Hit 10 chips. Walk up and look at how close each one finished.
  4. Count how many ended within 3 feet of the hole (one putter length is a good visual).
Why one club: We're measuring your current chipping reliability, not testing variety. Variety comes later.
Pitching = bigger swing than chipping:
  1. Pace off 30 yards from a target green (about 30 normal strides).
  2. Use your 56° or 60° (lob wedge) — whichever you usually grab from this distance.
  3. Hit 10 balls. Full pre-shot routine each time.
  4. Count how many finished within 6 feet of your target (about a 2-putter-length radius).
Chip vs. pitch: Chip = low, mostly roll, short swing. Pitch = higher, mostly fly, longer swing. 30 yards is squarely pitch territory.
What to do:
  1. Step off 50 yards from a green (about 50 normal strides).
  2. Use whichever wedge you'd grab on the course from 50 yards — usually 56° but could be 52° or 60°.
  3. Hit 10 balls with full routine.
  4. Count how many finished within 10 feet of your target (about 3 putter lengths).
Why 50 yards matters: This is "in-between" distance for most amateurs — not a full swing, not a chip. Getting good here is one of the biggest score-droppers in the game.
What to do:
  1. Find a practice bunker next to a green.
  2. Drop 5 balls in the sand, all from about the same lie (flat if possible).
  3. Use your 56° or 58°. Standard greenside splash — hit the sand 2 inches behind the ball.
  4. Count how many finished within 10 feet of the target.
  5. If there's no bunker available, skip and leave blank.
Note: If you don't usually practice bunker shots, this baseline number might be low. That's fine — it tells us we need to schedule bunker time.
Station 4
Range — Distance & Dispersion
45 minutes · Driving Range

Block-style: 10 quality balls per club. Don't rake-and-rip. Pick a target, full pre-shot routine, hit, evaluate, repeat. We need real distances and miss patterns.

For each club, record two things:
  • Carry distance: How far the ball flies in the air before hitting the ground. Use the range markers (usually every 25 or 50 yards) to estimate. Take an average across the 10 balls (ignore outliers).
  • Miss pattern: When you miss your target, which direction? Write one of: left, right, short, long, or a combo like "short-right". If 7 of 10 missed left, write "mostly left."
Why this matters: Your miss pattern tells me more about your swing than any distance number. Knowing your habitual miss = knowing what's actually wrong.
The 7-iron is golf's bellwether — long enough that swing flaws show up, short enough that most golfers can hit it solidly. How you hit your 7-iron is a strong predictor of how well you swing overall. Pay extra attention to where misses go.
Stock swing = your normal, repeatable, in-the-fairway swing. Not your "rip it" swing. Not your "show off to your buddies" swing. The swing you'd actually use on the first tee of a tournament. Goal: 10 swings that all look the same. Record where they tend to go.
Note shape too: Does it curve right-to-left (draw)? Left-to-right (fade)? Straight? Or a two-way miss?
Coach's note: If you don't know exact carry distances, that's the #1 data gap to fix this summer. Today, eyeball it from the range markers.
